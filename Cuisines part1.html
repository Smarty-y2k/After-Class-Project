Cuisines Of India

Indian food is also heavily influenced by religion, in particular Hinduism and Islam, cultural choices and traditions. Historical events such as invasions, trade relations, and colonialism have played a role in introducing certain foods to India. The Columbian discovery of the New World brought a number of new vegetables and fruits. A number of these such as potatoes, tomatoes, chillies, peanuts, and guava have become staples in many regions of India[3]
Indian cuisine has shaped the history of international relations; the spice trade between India and Europe was the primary catalyst for Europe's Age of Discovery. Spices were bought from India and traded around Europe and Asia. Indian cuisine has influenced other cuisines across the world, especially those from Europe (Britain in particular), the Middle East, Southern African, East Africa, Southeast Asia, North America, Mauritius, Fiji, Oceania, and the Caribbean.

FACTS

Spice Heaven: India is the world’s largest producer and consumer of spices.

Diverse Breads: From naan and roti to dosa and paratha, Indian cuisine has a vast variety of breads.

Vegetarian Paradise: India has the highest number of vegetarians in the world.

Tandoori Cooking: The tandoor (clay oven) dates back over 5,000 years.

Sweet Tooth: Indian sweets (mithai) like jalebi, gulab jamun, and barfi are famous worldwide.

Street Food King: India’s street food culture includes chaat, vada pav, pani puri, and kebabs.

In Indian mythology, Bheemasena, known for his strength and valor, is also depicted as an exceptional cook, credited with creating the "Aviyal" recipe, a thick vegetable stew. Mythological Chef: Bheemasena, a character from the epic Mahabharata, is celebrated not only for his strength but also for his culinary skills. Aviyal Creation: He is credited with creating the Aviyal, a thick and wholesome vegetable stew, a dish that is still popular in South Indian cuisine. Culinary Expertise: The story goes that during their exile, Bheemasena was forced to cook for Virata and, despite not being a trained chef, he created the Aviyal by chopping up vegetables and boiling them together. Other Mythological Chefs: Other characters in Indian mythology who are also known for their culinary skills include Sita and Draupadi. Nala Maharaja: Nala Maharaja is also considered an exceptional master chef.
